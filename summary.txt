	My goal for this project was to examine the impact of New York City’s new congestion zone tolling. My hypothesis was that although it would lead to a decrease in the number of vehicles entering the zone, it would lead to an increase in accident rates. To accomplish this, I used three data sets from the MTA. The first was a data set of all recorded collisions in NYC spanning back to 2014. The second was data from the congestion tolls to measure the number of vehicle entries; this data set was created on January 5, 2025, the day the tolls were implemented. The third data set was a chart from the MTA showing the average daily entries per month in 2025 and comparing them to the historical average to demonstrate the decrease in vehicle entries due to the tolls.
	I began by filtering the collision data. I sorted it into pre-toll and post-toll implementation, before and after January 5, 2025, as well as into collisions inside the congestion zone and outside the congestion zone, using geographical coordinates and street numbers. Next, I sorted the toll data so I could calculate daily entries. I used my own calculations rather than the MTA’s because I noticed that for their January calculation, they used the entire month, including days before January 5th, which skewed the results. I also noticed that their numbers included not only vehicles inside the congestion zone but also vehicles on excluded roadways, for example the FDR Drive, which is technically within the congestion zone but does not count toward tolls. Although it would have made more sense, and I had the ability, to subtract this data, I wanted to use their historical averages column, which required me to match how they collected the data. I used their historical averages instead of computing my own because there were no tolls historically, meaning there is no toll data from which I could calculate my own averages. Therefore, I calculated daily entries in the congestion zone after January 5, 2025, and used the MTA’s historical daily entries for comparison.
Then, using my sorted collision data, I separated it into 2025 collisions and 2024 collisions and calculated the monthly daily averages. Using my daily entry calculations and the MTA’s daily entry data, I calculated accident rates by dividing the number of collisions by the number of daily entries. The rates I calculated were minuscule because they were measured per individual vehicle, so I scaled the data by 100,000 to make it more meaningful. I then graphed the data: one chart showing accident rates per month, and another chart showing differences in entries, collisions, and accident rates between 2025 and 2024.
      My graphs and data portray my findings: the toll implementation actually increased accident rates. This is because even though there are fewer cars entering the zone, the number of accidents remains roughly the same, causing the rate to increase. I have a few theories as to why this occurred. One possibility is that reduced traffic density allows drivers to travel at higher speeds, leading to more severe crashes. Another possibility is that cost-conscious drivers, who may have been more cautious, are the ones most impacted by the toll pricing, meaning they are no longer driving in the zone and were not the primary contributors to accidents. Whatever the reason, the facts remain clear: congestion toll implementation increased accident rates, leading to the opposite of the intended effect.
      I also want to mention a few improvements that could have been made during data collection. Because the data set was uploaded to ADA in mid-November, the averages for November were inaccurate, and the data for December was nonexistent. If I were to re-upload the data sets at a later time, after December finished, I could provide a more complete picture of all of 2025 instead of just january through october. I also relied on MTA data that I did not calculate myself for historical daily entries. Lastly, it might have been helpful to control for weather-related accidents, but I did not do that.
